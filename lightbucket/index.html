Gazing in the night sky

May 4, 2016 | astronomy

About 6 months ago, I picked up a new hobby: Stargazing. I started with simply exploring and identifying constellations (Taurus, Orion and Canis Major) in the night sky. With a few week worth of such sessions, I was able to observe a few (some interesting and some obvious) things:

  • The International Space Station is frequently visible and the time/location can be accurately predicted.
  • The approximate flatness of the solar system. The and the first five planets seem to follow roughly the same path, suggesting a flat solar system. Particularly, on 22nd January 2016, were all aligned for the first time in over a decade.
  • Individual stars: Sirius (the brightest star), Betelgeuse (its visibly red!), Rigel and Planets: Jupiter, Venus (very bright), Mars (red), Saturn can be fun to look at individually.
  • Pleiades is an easy to identify cluster, with its 5-6 stars visible to the naked eye
  • Calculating the limiting magnitude. This may not be fun for everyone, but I like to calculate the limiting magnitude of the sky. Various charts are available for this. Limiting magnitude signifies how dim a star is visible (a higher LM means dimmer stars.
  • Satellite flares because of their reflective surfaces.
  • The Sun-Earth-Moon system. Knowing where the three are in relation to each other and hence the moon looks the way it looks.
  • The 22° halo around the moon is also an pretty sight.

I bought a telescope (similar to this one with better eyepieces) from a local shop after the naked eye sessions. Will write a few posts about my observations with the scope over the next few days.
